One of the lines I've said most often as a leader — and the one that tends to change the energy in a room fastest — is this:

"We may or may not be the problem. But we are the solution."

It's not about blame assignment. It's about power allocation.

## Why the Blame Question Is Usually the Wrong Question

When something goes wrong in an organizational context, the first response is almost always: whose fault is this? Who failed? Who should have caught it?

This question has a legitimate governance function — organizations need to understand root causes and accountability. But as an opening frame for the team that has to fix the problem, it's nearly useless.

The blame frame produces defensiveness, self-protection, and finger-pointing. It consumes the energy needed to solve the problem on solving a different question — the question of fault — that often has no clean answer and produces no useful output.

## The Reframe

The "we are the solution" frame does something specific: it returns agency to the people who have to do the work.

It doesn't deny fault. If the team made mistakes, those need to be understood — after the problem is addressed. The sequence matters: solve first, learn second. Blame later, or never.

What the reframe does is reset the mental model from victim (this happened to us) to agent (we are positioned to fix this). Victims explain. Agents execute.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

When a cross-functional problem lands on my team — a failure in a process we didn't design, a quality issue we inherited, a stakeholder dispute we walked into the middle of — my first move is to establish this frame explicitly.

I say: I don't know yet whose failure this was. That doesn't matter right now. What matters is that we are the team with the capability to address it, and we're going to.

I've watched this reframe change the energy in a team meeting within minutes. Not because the situation is better — it isn't. But because the frame shifts from explaining why this isn't our fault (defensive, reactive, low-energy) to demonstrating our capability to solve it (forward, active, high-energy).

The second frame is more accurate. In most organizational failures, causation is distributed and complex. Clean blame assignment is a fiction. But the capacity to solve the problem — that's specific, and it's often sitting right in the room.

## The Leadership Obligation

This reframe only works if the leader owns it first. I cannot ask my team to be solutions-oriented if I'm still oriented toward blame myself.

My job is to model the frame: acknowledge the difficulty, release the blame question, and focus the team on what they can actually control — the quality and speed of the response.

We may not be the problem. But we are the solution.

That's where I start. And it's where I lead.
