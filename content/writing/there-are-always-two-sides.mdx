I learned one of the most important lessons of my leadership career by being wrong about someone I thought I understood completely.

I had a stepdaughter I considered difficult — reactive, dramatic, causing problems with the rest of the family. I had a stepson I thought was steady and dependable. For years, I operated from that model.

Then, slowly, the real picture emerged. The steady one had been quietly positioning the difficult one — managing perceptions, shaping narratives, creating the appearance I had accepted as fact.

There were always two sides. I had only heard one.

I carry that lesson into every leadership situation I've been in since.

## Why Leaders Stop at One Side

It's faster. The first account of a conflict is usually vivid, detailed, and emotionally compelling. The person who gets to you first has a natural advantage — they've framed the situation, assigned the roles, and made you feel like you already understand what happened.

Moving to resolution from that position feels efficient. It's not. It's expensive.

The cost is trust — specifically, the trust of the person whose side you didn't hear. And once people learn that you make judgments before you listen, they stop bringing you their real problems.

## The Listening Framework I Use

When a conflict or dispute lands on my desk, I follow a consistent sequence:

**Step 1: Hear the first account fully.** I don't interrupt. I don't ask leading questions. I let the full story land. I take notes on specifics: what happened, when, who was involved, what the outcome was.

**Step 2: Ask exactly one clarifying question.** "Is there anything else I should know before I talk to the other person?" This signals that I'm going to talk to the other person, and that the account isn't complete yet.

**Step 3: Hear the second account with the same discipline.** Full attention. No interruptions. No "well, the other person said..." I want the uncontaminated version.

**Step 4: Find the overlap.** Almost every conflict has a factual center that both parties agree on, surrounded by interpretation and emotion. I look for what's agreed upon first. That's the ground I stand on.

**Step 5: Respond — not react.** With the full picture, I can now address the actual problem rather than the first-account version of it.

## What This Does for Your Authority

The leaders I've seen command the most genuine respect — not the respect that comes from title or fear, but the kind that makes people bring you their real problems — are the ones known for their fairness.

Fairness isn't splitting the difference. It's giving every person the confidence that their side of the story will be heard before a judgment is made.

When people know you listen before you conclude, they stop protecting themselves from you. They start relying on you.

---

There are always two sides. The second one is often the more important one.

Listen for it. Lead from it.
