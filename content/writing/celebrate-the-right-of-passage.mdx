Every team I've ever built has a version of this moment: a new team member, still learning the environment, does something for the first time. They figure out a configuration that's been tripping everyone up. They navigate a process that confuses new hires. They break the code.

How the leader responds in that moment matters more than almost anything else that happens in the first 90 days.

## What Most Leaders Do

Most leaders acknowledge competent performance as a baseline. "Good job. Next ticket."

This is a missed opportunity of the highest order.

## What the Moment Actually Is

The first time someone on your team does something they didn't know they could do, they are at their maximum receptivity to feedback about identity. They're in the moment where they can be helped to say: I am the kind of person who can do this.

That transition — from "I tried the thing" to "I am someone who does this" — is where expertise begins. Not in the doing, but in the recognition that the doing is becoming part of who they are.

The leader who names it explicitly — "this is the moment, right here, where you became the person who handles this" — is doing more than managing performance. They're shaping identity.

## How I Do This

I stop. Visibly, deliberately, conspicuously. Not a quick "good job" in passing. I name the achievement with specificity.

"Jordan just figured out how the content fragment rollout actually works across multi-site management. That's something that confuses developers who've been here for a year. I want everyone to know: when you have a question about that, Jordan is who you ask now."

Three things happen simultaneously:

**Jordan receives public recognition** that installs a professional identity, not just a compliment.

**The rest of the team learns** that expertise gets recognized here — that becoming the authority on something has visible, social value.

**The culture signal gets sent**: this team celebrates the right of passage. We treat first achievements as meaningful events, not just completed tickets.

## The Constructive Teaching Moment

There's a second element: I always use the moment to name what made the achievement possible. Not the luck or the talent — the thinking process, the persistence, the question that unlocked the answer.

"The reason Jordan figured this out was that she kept asking the edge case question instead of accepting the surface answer. That's how you break through on things that look impossible."

Now it's not just praise. It's instruction. Every other team member has learned something about how to navigate the next hard problem.

## The Culture It Builds

Teams that celebrate the right of passage develop a specific property: new people ramp up faster and with less anxiety.

Because everyone knows: when I figure something out, the team will notice. And when I'm stuck, there's someone who's been through it.

That's the culture worth building. It starts with one deliberate moment, repeated consistently, until the team believes it.
