Every performance review I've ever given includes a conversation that doesn't appear on any standard rubric.

I ask: did you get this done the right way?

Not "did you deliver on time" — though that matters. Not "did you meet the quality standard" — though that matters too. But something more fundamental: did you maintain a healthy work environment with the people you worked with? Did you honor your own values in how you executed? Were you ethical in your performance? Did you game the system to create the appearance of success?

Those questions aren't soft. They're the hardest ones to answer honestly.

## The Metrics Miss the Point

Organizations measure output because output is measurable. Lines of code. Pages migrated. Tickets closed. Campaign impressions. Deals closed. These numbers are real and they tell you something.

But they don't tell you what it cost to produce them.

I've watched teams hit every metric while quietly destroying themselves. Hit rates maintained through pressure that burned out three senior contributors. Migration numbers achieved by cutting QA corners that the next team inherited as technical debt. Adoption rates driven by political maneuvering that fractured the cross-functional relationships needed for the next initiative.

The numbers looked great. The organization paid for them for years.

## The Standard I Hold

My standard has four parts:

**Did you deliver results?** Yes — results matter. Excellence produces outcomes. I hold the team to high expectations, and I expect them to be met.

**Did you maintain the environment?** The team culture is a shared resource. Every interaction either deposits into it or withdraws from it. Was the environment in better shape after your involvement than before it?

**Did you honor your values?** This is the personal one — the question that only the individual can answer honestly. Did you operate with integrity? Were you truthful when it would have been easier not to be? Did you do what was right when doing what was expedient was available?

**Did you build something that lasts?** Or did you ship something that works today and breaks in six months? Did you create documentation, training, processes that someone else can build on? Did you develop the people around you?

## Why This Standard Matters for Careers

This isn't just about being a good person. It's about building a career that's worth having.

The professionals I've watched thrive over decades are not the ones who optimized for the number at any cost. They're the ones who built reputations for doing good work the right way. For being someone you could trust. For leaving teams, codebases, and organizations in better condition than they found them.

That reputation compounds. It opens doors that credentials don't.

---

Results matter. They always will. But the how is what you carry with you.

How you get things done is the measure that follows you from role to role, organization to organization, year to year.

Get the result. Do it right.
