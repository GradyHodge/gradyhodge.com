My soul resonates at its highest level when I recognize and implement a unique solution to a problem that makes life's purpose easier and more enjoyable.

That sentence is in my personal values document. I've read it hundreds of times. Every time, it's more accurate than the time before.

Creative solutions are not my hobby. They're my highest professional value — and I want to explain what that actually means in practice.

## What Creative Solutions Are Not

Creative solutions are not novelty for its own sake. I'm not interested in building something unusual because unusual is interesting. That's a junior mistake.

Creative solutions are also not complexity. The most elegant solutions I've ever implemented looked simple to the end user and deceptively simple to anyone reviewing the code. The complexity was in the thinking that produced the simplicity.

And creative solutions are not the first idea. The first idea is almost always the obvious one. The obvious solution is almost always the one everyone else will implement — which means the problem will recur in exactly the same way.

## What They Are

A creative solution is a response to a problem that is:

**Non-obvious.** It required more thinking than the obvious path. It considered the problem from an angle that wasn't immediately apparent.

**Precisely fitted.** It solves this problem, for these constraints, in this context — not a generic answer that happens to work here.

**Generative.** A great creative solution doesn't just resolve the current problem — it opens up new capabilities or prevents adjacent problems.

## How I Find Them

The pattern I've noticed in my best work: the creative solution usually emerges after I've resisted the first two obvious answers.

When I was coordinating 65 developers across 20 product teams, the obvious answer to publishing conflicts was process enforcement: rules about who could push to production, when, in what sequence. That works. It also creates friction and delays.

The creative solution — one I brought back from Adobe Summit — was a communication layer built on top of the WCM that displayed who last worked on a page and whether it was currently being edited. Transparent, real-time, no enforcement required. The behavior changed because the information was visible, not because the rules were stricter.

That solution didn't come from the first 20 minutes of thinking. It came from asking: what if enforcement isn't the right lever? What if this is an information problem masquerading as a compliance problem?

## The Practice

Creative solutions require patience with ambiguity. You have to sit with the problem longer than it's comfortable to sit with it. You have to resist the pull of the good-enough answer.

I think of ideas no one has ever thought of before as one of my life's purposes. Not because I have special powers of imagination — but because I'm willing to think longer, consider more angles, and question the frame of the problem itself.

That's a practice, not a talent. And it's available to anyone willing to do the work.
