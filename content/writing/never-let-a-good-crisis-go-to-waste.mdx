Winston Churchill delivered some version of this line to the British people at the worst moment of World War II — cities being bombed, casualties mounting, the end unclear.

"Never let a good crisis go to waste."

Most people interpret this as cynical opportunism. I interpret it as one of the most operationally useful leadership principles I've ever encountered.

Here's what it means in practice — and why the crises in your professional life may be the most valuable things that happen to you.

## What a Crisis Actually Creates

A crisis is, above everything else, a concentrator of attention and authority. In normal operating conditions, the changes that need to happen — the process overhauls, the structural redesigns, the cultural resets — are slowed by inertia, competing priorities, and the human tendency to leave things that technically work alone.

A crisis removes those barriers. Stakeholders who would never approve a major process change under normal circumstances will approve it when the current process has visibly failed. Colleagues who resist structural changes will welcome them when the alternative is clearly worse.

The crisis creates a window. The question is whether you're prepared to move through it.

## How I've Applied This

At U.S. Bank, my team was suddenly assigned responsibility for WCAG accessibility compliance across 5,000 live pages — without additional resources, without a clear timeline, and without any existing process for handling it at scale.

That was a crisis. The instinct was to defend against the scope: push back on the timeline, negotiate down the requirement, wait for clarity that wasn't coming.

My response: treat it as an opportunity. Build the process from scratch, at scale, that we should have had all along. Train the team systematically on accessibility standards, create testing protocols, establish QA workflows. Turn a mandate that landed as a burden into the foundation of a capability that made the team more valuable.

We delivered. The team came out of it with skills, processes, and credibility we wouldn't have had without the pressure.

## The Requirement: Preparation

The reason most people cannot benefit from crises is that they haven't done the thinking in advance. When the crisis hits, they're in emergency mode — reactive, defensive, trying to limit damage.

Crisis preparation is not disaster preparedness. It's the ongoing practice of knowing what you'd do if the current approach failed. What would you change if you had the authority? What process would you rebuild if it broke completely?

When the crisis arrives and the window opens, you need the answer ready.

## The Reframe

Every crisis I've navigated has left something behind that I wouldn't have had without it. Not always immediately visible — sometimes the gain takes months to surface. But it's there.

The skill, the relationship, the process improvement, the strategic clarity. The obstacles that felt like setbacks were also — simultaneously — the most valuable learning events of my career.

Never let a good crisis go to waste. Have your answer ready before the window opens.
