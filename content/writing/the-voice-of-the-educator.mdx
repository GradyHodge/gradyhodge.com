I've taught every age group from three years old to eighty-nine. Not all of them were in classrooms or conference rooms — some were in living rooms, some were on the street, some were in coma recovery. The range is wide enough that I've had to learn one thing deeply: understanding how someone learns is more important than knowing what you're teaching.

That insight has shaped how I communicate in every professional context since.

## The Three Layers of Technical Communication

When I explain a technical system — an AI architecture, a content management workflow, an accessibility standard, a data pipeline — I move through three layers, in order:

**Layer 1: The problem it solves.** Not the technology, not the implementation — the human problem that motivated building it. "This exists because someone was experiencing [X] and needed a better way." Starting from the problem orients the listener before any technical information arrives.

**Layer 2: The mental model.** A simplified, accurate representation of how the system works. Not complete — useful. A metaphor or analogy that creates an accurate intuition without requiring full technical depth. "Think of it like a librarian who has read everything but can only answer questions they've been asked."

**Layer 3: The interaction surface.** What you need to understand to use this effectively. Not the full implementation — the inputs, outputs, and decisions that are yours to make.

Most technical communicators skip to Layer 3. The people in the room nod and leave having understood nothing they'll be able to act on.

## Adapting to the Learner

I adapt my teaching style to the person in front of me. Not a performance — a genuine recalibration of what frame will land.

With a senior developer: I skip the analogy and go deep on implementation decisions. They don't need the simplified model — they want the tradeoffs.

With an executive: I stay at Layer 1 almost entirely. What problem does this solve, what does success look like, what decision do they need to make?

With a new team member: I go slowly through all three layers, check understanding explicitly, and make it safe to ask the question twice.

The mistake most experts make: they stay in the mode that's comfortable for them, not the one that serves the learner.

## The Educator's Obligation

My deepest desire is to share what is in my heart and soul by any means — and I have a natural ability to understand complex systems and adapt my teaching style to the understanding of my students.

That ability is a responsibility. Not just a professional tool — an obligation to make the knowledge available to the person who needs it, in the form they can actually receive.

The knowledge that stays in your head is the knowledge that doesn't compound. Share it. Teach it. Watch what happens when the people around you understand at the level you do.

That's when the real work becomes possible.
