There's a category of professional I've worked with my entire career who spends most of their time putting out fires. They're good at it. They move fast, they're responsive, they handle crises with competence. And they're exhausted.

The reason they're exhausted: they're solving instances of problems rather than solving the structure that produces them.

Systems thinking changes this. Not instantly — but permanently.

## What Systems Thinking Actually Is

A system is a set of elements, interconnected by relationships, producing a function over time. Every organization is a system. Every team is a system. Every product is a system.

Systems thinking is the practice of analyzing problems at the level of structure — not incident. When something goes wrong repeatedly, a systems thinker asks: what is the structure producing this pattern? What relationships, incentives, or feedback loops are creating this outcome over and over?

The fire-putter-outer asks: how do I handle this fire? The systems thinker asks: why do fires keep appearing here?

## The Four Levers Are a Systems Model

My leadership framework — people, process, platform, performance — is a systems model. When a team is underperforming, you don't diagnose the symptom. You diagnose which of the four systems is producing the symptom.

Bad outcomes from unclear ownership? That's a process problem. Recurring quality failures? Could be people, could be platform, could be a performance standard that's not explicit. High attrition? Almost always a people system failure — hiring for credentials over culture, or a process system failure — unclear growth paths.

Fix the system. The symptom resolves.

## Applied: How I Fixed a Publishing Conflict Problem

At U.S. Bank, we had recurring publishing conflicts across 5,000 pages. The instinct was to fix each conflict as it happened — identify what was overwritten, restore the correct version, document the incident.

A systems analysis revealed the structure: multiple teams editing the same pages in the same content management system with no visibility into concurrent activity. The conflicts weren't a discipline problem. They weren't a communication problem. They were a visibility problem built into the system's architecture.

The fix: a communication layer showing real-time editing status on every page. The incident rate dropped immediately. Not because people worked harder or followed better rules — because the system no longer produced the conflict conditions.

## The Leverage Point

Systems thinking changes where you invest effort. The highest-leverage interventions are almost always structural. Changing an incentive system produces more lasting change than changing individual behavior. Redesigning a process produces more lasting change than training people to work around a broken one.

The question to ask when a problem recurs: what is the structure producing this? Not who failed. Not what was misunderstood. What is the structure?

Find that structure. Change it. The problem stops recurring.

That's leverage. That's how one engineer with the right mental model outproduces a whole team treating symptoms.
