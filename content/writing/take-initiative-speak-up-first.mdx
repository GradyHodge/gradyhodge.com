There's a famous study in psychology about diffusion of responsibility. When one person witnesses an emergency, they help 70% of the time. When five people witness the same emergency, the help rate drops to 20% — because everyone assumes someone else will handle it.

The professional equivalent of this is the meeting where everyone can see the problem, no one raises it, and the group leaves 45 minutes later having committed to a plan that's going to fail in a way that was visible before they started.

I don't wait. I speak first.

## Why Most People Don't Speak First

The risk is real: being wrong, being dismissed, being seen as the difficult one who slows things down. The social cost of raising an uncomfortable issue in a group setting is not nothing.

But the alternative cost — the cost of proceeding with a plan everyone had private doubts about — is almost always higher.

My observation: in most rooms, when I raise the issue that's sitting unspoken in the room, at least 60% of the people there are privately grateful. They saw the same thing. They weren't willing to say it first.

Speaking first is a service to the room. Not heroism — function.

## The Practice

I've made it a discipline: when I see a problem, I name it. Specifically, without drama, without blame assignment. "I want to flag something before we move forward." 

Then I state what I see, as precisely as I can. Not an editorial — an observation. Here is what I notice. Here is the concern. Here is the question I'd want answered before I'd feel confident about this direction.

Then I stop talking. I've said the thing. The room has the information. What happens next is collaborative.

This is different from being the contrarian who blocks everything with objections. The goal is not to slow the process — it's to expose the problem before the process is irreversible.

## The Standard I Learned the Hard Way

The worst place to have a heart attack is on the sidewalk of a busy New York City street. Everyone assumes someone else is handling it. The bystander effect kills.

The professional equivalent: the team that could have caught the problem in week one didn't say anything, and now you're six months in with a budget overrun and a stakeholder who's lost confidence.

The person who speaks first in week one is uncomfortable for 45 minutes. The person who waits until month six is uncomfortable for a year.

Speak up first. Name the concern. Give the room the chance to address it before it becomes a crisis.

You don't have to have the solution when you raise the problem. You just have to be willing to raise it.

That's the discipline. And it saves more projects than any technical skill I've ever had.
