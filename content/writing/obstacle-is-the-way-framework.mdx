Marcus Aurelius wrote it in the second century: the impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way.

Ryan Holiday made this famous again in 2014. I've been applying it — not as a motivational idea, but as a literal problem-solving framework — for years.

Here's what that looks like in practice.

## The First Move: Reframe the Obstacle as Information

Most people encounter an obstacle and ask: how do I get past this? That's the wrong question. It assumes the obstacle is external to the solution.

The right question is: what is this obstacle telling me about the problem I'm actually trying to solve?

Every significant blocker I've encountered in professional work turned out to be information I didn't have before. The stakeholder who won't approve the budget isn't an obstacle to the project — they're telling me something about organizational priorities or trust levels that I need to understand. The technical limitation that prevents the obvious implementation isn't an obstacle to the product — it's a constraint that, understood correctly, points toward a more elegant solution.

The obstacle is not in the way. It is the way, because it contains the information needed to find the real path.

## The Second Move: Refuse to Waste the Crisis

Winston Churchill told the British people at the height of World War II — when their cities were being bombed and they were exhausted and near collapse — "never let a good crisis go to waste."

That wasn't callousness. It was strategic clarity: adversity creates opportunity that comfort never produces. Crises expose weaknesses that should have been addressed earlier. They create urgency that makes previously impossible decisions suddenly possible. They clarify priorities with brutal efficiency.

Every professional crisis I've navigated — team dysfunction, platform failures, scope disasters, stakeholder conflicts — produced the best process improvements I've ever implemented. Not despite the crisis, but because of it.

## The Third Move: Grow Stronger, Not Bitter

This is the hardest part. Obstacles, especially unfair ones, create the temptation to become protective, defensive, and smaller. The professional trauma response: hedge everything, commit to nothing, trust no one.

The better response — the harder one — is to extract the learning and grow forward. Ask: what capability do I now have that I didn't have before this obstacle? What would have broken me earlier that I can navigate now?

This requires an honest accounting of the cost. The obstacle was real. The difficulty was real. The growth on the other side is also real.

## What This Produces

The leaders and contributors I most admire have all been shaped by significant obstacles. Not untouched by them — shaped by them. Their judgment is better. Their resilience is deeper. Their solutions are more creative because they've learned that the obvious path is rarely the best one.

The obstacle is the way. It always has been.

The only question is whether you're willing to walk through it deliberately — or wait until you have no other choice.
