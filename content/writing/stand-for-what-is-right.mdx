I cannot sit idly by and not be the first to take initiative to address the needs of those around me.

That sentence is in my core values document. I've tested it enough times to know it's accurate — and to understand the cost and the reward of living by it.

The cost: you become the person who raises the uncomfortable issue, who pushes back on the convenient consensus, who asks the question that changes the meeting's direction in ways not everyone wanted. You carry a reputation as someone who won't just go along.

The reward: you become the person people trust when the stakes are real.

## What the Paladin Actually Does

In my personal values framework, I call this the Paladin archetype: the leader who acts as a man of action for justice. Not impulsive or aggressive — measured, deliberate, but unwilling to stay quiet when staying quiet means allowing something unjust or wrong to proceed unchallenged.

In professional terms, this looks like:

- Raising the concern everyone sees but no one names
- Defending a team member who is being blamed unfairly in a cross-functional dispute
- Pushing back on a strategic decision that will produce harm I can see coming
- Saying the thing that will cost me standing in the room but is clearly true

None of these are heroic in the dramatic sense. They're functional. They require a specific kind of courage — not physical, but social. The willingness to accept the friction that comes from saying the right thing when the easy thing is to stay quiet.

## The Distinction That Matters

Standing for what is right is not the same as having strong opinions loosely held. It's not stubbornness, or contrarianism, or the need to be the person who asks hard questions for its own sake.

The Paladin acts from principle, not personality. The question is not "what do I prefer" but "what is right" — and then having the courage to act on the answer.

I also know there are times when I'm wrong. Standing for what is right requires genuine inquiry, not just conviction. Two sides exist to every story. I hear both before I act on either.

But once I'm clear — once I've heard the full picture and I know what's right — I act.

## The Professional Implication

In my experience, teams with a Paladin — someone who can be relied upon to say the right thing when it needs to be said — operate differently from teams without one.

The energy in those teams is cleaner. People don't have to manage the political calculus of every statement because they know someone in the room will call out what's actually happening. The dishonesty that corrodes culture doesn't accumulate, because it gets named.

I bend and break arbitrary rules that exist only to harm rather than protect. I give my time, talents, and energy to do what is right.

That's not idealism. It's a professional standard.

Hold it. The teams you build will be better for it.
