The default career is reactive. An opportunity appears. You take it. Another opportunity appears. You take it. Ten years later, you have a résumé that tells a coherent story only in retrospect — and sometimes not even then.

The deliberate career is designed. Not rigidly — careers require adaptation. But designed in the sense that there is a specific version of yourself you're building toward, and every major career decision gets evaluated against that direction.

Here's the framework I use — and why I believe it produces dramatically different outcomes.

## Start with the Identity, Not the Role

The conventional career planning question is: what do you want to do? That's the wrong starting question.

The right question is: who do you want to become?

Roles change. Technologies change. Industries change. The identity you're building — the specific set of capabilities, values, and expertise that defines who you are professionally — is more stable. And it's a better north star for the thousand small decisions that make up a career.

For me: I am becoming an AI engineer and consultant who builds the conditions for human flourishing inside organizations and technology systems. That identity contains both the technical dimension (AI engineering, full-stack development) and the leadership dimension (eudaimonia architecture, SME development, organizational culture).

Every project I take on, every skill I develop, every role I consider — I evaluate against that identity. Does this build toward who I'm becoming, or does it pull me away from it?

## Map the Gap

Once you know who you're becoming, you can map the gap: what capabilities, credentials, and experiences does the future version of you have that the current version doesn't?

That gap is your curriculum. Not a wish list of things you'd like to do someday — a specific inventory of the development required to close the distance between where you are and where you're going.

I map mine explicitly: the AI engineering skills I'm building, the leadership portfolio I'm developing, the product deliveries that constitute evidence of capability. Each item on the map is connected to a project I'm executing or planning.

## The Career Portfolio as Navigation Tool

The career portfolio isn't just marketing. It's a navigation instrument.

Every project you document forces you to answer: does this demonstrate the expertise I'm building toward? Is this evidence of the capabilities that define the professional identity I'm constructing?

If the answer is no — if a project would add to your résumé but not to your identity narrative — that's valuable information. Not necessarily a reason to decline, but a reason to be honest about what it's costing you in attention and time.

## The Identity Shift as Career Leap

The largest career advances I've made have come from identity shifts, not credential accumulations. The moment I stopped seeing myself as an AEM author who understood AI and started seeing myself as an AI engineer who had deep enterprise content experience, the opportunities that appeared changed.

Your identity precedes your opportunity. Change who you are becoming. The market responds.
