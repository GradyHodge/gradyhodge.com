title: The 5 Types of Safety Every Leader Must Create
description: Most leaders think safety means hard hats and fire exits. There are four more types — and they're the ones that determine whether your team flourishes or quietly disengages.
date: '2026-04-07'

When most people hear "workplace safety," they think hard hats and fire exits. That's physical safety — and yes, it matters. But in 16 years of leading teams, I've come to understand that physical safety is just the entry point. There are four more types that determine whether your team will merely survive — or genuinely flourish.

Here are the five types of safety that define a high-performance, human-centered team.

## 1. Physical Safety

The foundation. Every team member needs to feel physically secure — no hostile environments, no threats, no unsafe conditions. This is table stakes. If you can't get this one right, nothing else matters.

## 2. Emotional Safety

This is where most leaders fail. Emotional safety means your team can bring uncertainty, confusion, and even failure to you without fear of ridicule, punishment, or dismissal. It means someone can say "I don't understand this" without being made to feel inadequate. It means mistakes are treated as data, not character flaws.

When I was managing 65 developers, I watched what happened when emotional safety was absent: people stopped asking questions. They guessed. They hid their mistakes. The downstream costs were enormous.

My three-word standard: be kind, be patient, be professional. It sounds simple. Most teams have never experienced it consistently.

## 3. Intellectual Safety

Intellectual safety means every person on the team — regardless of title or tenure — feels their perspective is worth sharing. Their ideas get heard. Their questions get answered. Their expertise gets utilized.

I formalize this through Subject Matter Expert (SME) assignments. When someone becomes the recognized authority on a specific domain, they stop holding back. They lean in. They grow. And the whole team benefits from the upgrade.

## 4. Financial Safety

People cannot focus on excellence when they're worried about their income. As a leader, you may not control compensation directly — but you can be transparent about stability, advocate for your team's financial interests, and remove the anxiety of uncertainty whenever possible.

Security produces performance. Anxiety produces self-protection. You cannot have both simultaneously.

## 5. Social Safety

Your team exists in relationship to other teams, leadership, clients, and stakeholders. Social safety means: I have your back. When cross-functional disputes erupt, when someone gets blamed unfairly, when politics threaten your team member's reputation — you have a leader who will defend them, represent them, and make sure the right story gets told.

My standard is explicit: if you're kind, patient, and professional in a conflict, I will represent you without hesitation. That promise changes how people show up.

---

Five types of safety. Most leaders manage one. The best leaders architect all five deliberately.

When all five are present, something remarkable happens. People stop protecting themselves and start building. That's eudaimonia — not a concept, a condition. And it's something you can engineer.

The workplace you want is not the result of good intentions. It's the result of deliberate design.

Aristotle coined eudaimonia over 2,300 years ago. Most people translate it as "happiness." That's an inadequate translation. A more accurate rendering: the state of flourishing that comes from living and working according to your highest capacities.

I've built my entire leadership philosophy around making that ancient idea operational inside modern teams.

## The Problem with Modern Workplace Culture

Most organizations optimize for performance metrics while neglecting the conditions that make sustained high performance possible. They focus on output without asking: are these people becoming better? Are they developing? Are they being seen and utilized at their highest level?

The result is what I call the slow drain: talented people gradually disengaging, protecting themselves rather than contributing, doing just enough to keep their jobs rather than doing the best work of their lives.

You don't fix that with team-building exercises. You fix it by engineering the right conditions.

## What Eudaimonia Looks Like on a Team

A team experiencing eudaimonia exhibits specific, observable behaviors:

- People ask questions freely — even the embarrassing ones
- Mistakes get reported immediately rather than concealed
- Team members advocate for each other across organizational boundaries
- People are growing toward the next level of their career, not stagnating
- The best ideas come from anywhere in the org chart, not just from managers

These aren't accidents. They're the result of deliberate leadership choices made consistently over time.

## How I Build It

My first conversation with any new team member always includes this question: "If you don't feel like what you're doing right now is preparing you for the next level of your career, I need to know. We'll update your goals together."

That one question signals three things simultaneously: I see you as a whole person with a career arc. I'm invested in your growth beyond this role. And your development is a shared responsibility.

From there, I systematically identify expertise within the team — skills people already have, skills they want to build, gaps the team needs to fill — and I create formal SME designations. Someone becomes the recognized authority. They teach. They get credit. They grow.

## The Leader's Role

Eudaimonia doesn't happen by itself. It requires a leader willing to do two difficult things simultaneously: hold the team to a high standard of excellence while protecting the environment that makes excellence possible.

Remove ego. Remove fear. Remove the politics that drain energy.

Build safety. Build expertise. Build trust.

That's the job. Not managing metrics. Not executing strategy. Engineering the conditions in which human beings become their best selves.

I've watched it work at scale — 65 developers, 5,000 pages, 20 product teams. The teams that flourished weren't the ones with the best talent. They were the ones with the best conditions.

Give people those conditions. The performance follows.

The best thing I've ever done as a leader is give people problems that are slightly too big for them — and then make sure they had everything they needed to solve them.

That's the SME model in one sentence. Here's what it looks like in practice.

## Why Most Teams Underutilize Their Talent

Most managers assume expertise has to be imported. If there's a problem, they hire a specialist. If there's a gap, they look outside the team. The internal talent gets left to execute instructions rather than develop authority.

This creates two compounding problems: the team stops growing, and the manager becomes the permanent bottleneck on every complex decision.

I've led teams of 65 people across enterprise technology deployments. The biggest gains I saw never came from hiring better people — they came from developing the people I already had into recognized authorities.

## How the SME Program Works

The first step is a talent inventory. I don't just look at job descriptions — I ask. What do you already know deeply? What do you want to learn? What problems on this team would you be excited to own?

The answers are almost always surprising. The person managing content permissions is secretly a metadata taxonomy expert. The junior developer has spent years studying accessibility standards. The quiet one in the back has strong opinions about Jira workflows that turn out to be exactly right.

Once identified, I formalize it. I assign the domain. I give them the title internally. And then — critically — I step back.

## Give Them the Platform, Then Get Out of the Way

The SME program only works if the leader does something counterintuitive: publicly defer to the expert they created.

In meetings, I direct questions to the SME. In cross-functional discussions, I bring the SME to the table. When their domain comes up in leadership briefings, I say the name and give them the credit. Explicitly. Every time.

This accomplishes two things. The person grows into their authority — they rise to meet the expectation you've set publicly. And the rest of the team learns that expertise gets recognized here. That knowledge is currency. That learning is rewarded.

## The Multiplication Effect

At U.S. Bank, I built a formal SME program across a team of 65 AEM developers. Accessibility became its own domain. DAM governance became its own domain. Jira reporting became its own domain. Advanced component training became its own domain.

The team stopped waiting for me to answer questions. They asked each other. They created training materials for new hires. They solved problems I never would have had the bandwidth to solve myself.

## The Standard I Keep

I don't hoard expertise. I don't become the permanent expert in the room. My job is to make myself unnecessary — by making everyone around me exceptional.

The measure of my leadership isn't the work I produce. It's the number of subject matter experts I created before I left.

That's the standard. And it scales wherever you take it.

Early in my leadership career, I spent a lot of energy on things I couldn't control: organizational politics, budget decisions, executive priorities that shifted without warning. I was frustrated constantly, convinced that the results I wanted were being blocked by forces outside my influence.

Then I stopped. I asked a different question: what can I actually control?

The answer turned out to be exactly four things.

## The 4 Levers

**People. Process. Platform. Performance.**

Every result a team produces flows through one of these four channels. Every problem a team has can be traced back to a weakness in one of them. And — this is the key insight — every single one of them is within the leader's direct influence.

## Lever 1: People

The people on your team are the highest-leverage variable you have. Hiring well is the most important decision a leader makes. But hiring well isn't just about credentials — it's about character.

My standard: hire for heart over talent every time. Find someone good enough who is hungry and motivated to grow, and you'll outperform the brilliant person who stopped caring.

Beyond hiring, people management means developing the individuals already on your team. Creating SMEs. Identifying who's ready for a stretch assignment. Recognizing who needs a different kind of support. People management is active, not passive.

## Lever 2: Process

Process is the architecture of how work gets done. Bad process doesn't just slow things down — it demoralizes people. It makes them feel like the organization is working against them.

When I inherited a team of 65 AEM developers coordinating across 20 product teams, the process was broken. Jira filters were useless. No one knew who was working on what. Publishing conflicts were constant.

Process is a lever. Pull it deliberately.

Your team needs the right tools. A great developer using the wrong platform is still producing less than they should. An excellent content team fighting their CMS is burning energy they should be putting into their work.

As a leader, your job is to advocate for the right platforms, configure them correctly, and train your team to use them at full capacity. When I attended Adobe Summit in 2023, I came back with an implementation that prevented publishing conflicts for 5,000 live pages. That was a platform lever — pulled correctly.

## Lever 4: Performance

Performance isn't just output metrics — it's the quality of how work gets done. Did the team maintain their standards? Did they act with integrity? Did they honor the work environment they're trying to build?

I track performance not just numerically but behaviorally. Are people growing? Are they using what they've learned? Are they operating at a level that reflects their potential?

---

Four levers. They're all yours. The leaders who produce consistent, excellent results are the ones who pull all four — deliberately, regularly, and without waiting for permission.

You don't need more authority to lead. You need to use the leverage you already have.

**Be kind. Be patient. Be professional.**

That's it. That was the standard I set — and the promise I made to back it up.

## Why Three Words Work

Management philosophies fail when they're too abstract to evaluate against in the moment. In the middle of a conflict — a publishing error, a scope dispute, a stakeholder complaint — no one is consulting a values document. They're reacting.

Three words are different. They're specific enough to be a real-time test. In the heat of a cross-functional dispute, I could ask: am I being kind right now? Am I being patient? Am I conducting myself professionally?

## What These Words Actually Mean

**Kind** doesn't mean nice. It means you treat the person across from you as a human being with a legitimate perspective, even when you disagree with their position or their approach. Kindness in a conflict is the hardest thing — and the highest leverage.

**Patient** means you wait for the whole picture before you respond. You ask clarifying questions. You let people finish. You resist the pull toward fast conclusions. Patience is an act of respect — it signals that what the other person is saying is worth hearing completely.

**Professional** means you separate the person from the problem. You don't make it personal. You don't raise your voice. You don't resort to politics or positioning. You stay in solution mode, even when the situation is emotional.

## The Promise I Keep

The three-word standard only worked because I enforced it in one direction: toward my team. When someone on my team was dragged into a cross-functional conflict, my first question wasn't "what did they do wrong?" It was "did they apply the standard?"

If they did — if they were kind, patient, and professional — they had my full support. I would walk into that stakeholder meeting and represent them without hesitation.

That promise changed the culture. People stopped defending themselves through politics and started trusting that excellence and integrity had a protector.

---

And it gives me a clear standard to lead by.

Try it in your next difficult conversation. Be kind. Be patient. Be professional. See what happens when you stop reacting and start responding.

There's a scarcity mindset in most organizations around credit. Leaders accumulate it. They attribute wins upward to themselves and failures downward to their teams. They treat recognition as a finite resource that must be hoarded.

I operate from the opposite assumption. Credit is generative. The more you give away, the more you produce.

Here's what I mean.

## The Hidden Cost of Credit Hoarding

When leaders take credit for their team's work, something invisible happens: the team starts to hold back. The best idea gets kept quiet because "why bother — he'll take credit for it anyway." The extra mile doesn't get walked because "no one's going to notice it was me."

Over time, you end up with a team that executes instructions rather than generates initiative. You become the permanent bottleneck. You work harder. The team works less hard. And the quality ceiling drops to whatever you can personally produce.

That's not leverage. That's a trap.

## What Giving Credit Actually Does

When I publicly attribute a win to the person who actually produced it — by name, with specifics, in the meeting where it matters — several things happen simultaneously.

That person grows into the attribution. Human beings have a tendency to meet the expectation that's been set publicly for them. When I say "Marcus solved this problem — let me have him walk you through it," Marcus becomes the person who solves that kind of problem. That's not just perception management. That's identity development in real time.

The rest of the team pays attention. They learn that expertise gets recognized here. That initiative gets attributed. That being known for solving something valuable is a real outcome, not a fantasy.

And I get something back that's worth far more than a single win: loyalty, initiative, and a team that's actively looking for the next problem to solve.

## The SME Credit Loop

This is why my SME program isn't just about assigning domains — it's about making sure the credit for those domains is visibly tracked back to the person who owns them.

When an accessibility issue gets solved in a cross-functional meeting, I don't say "my team handled it." I say: "Jordan has been our WCAG subject matter expert for the past six months — Jordan, walk us through what we found."

Jordan walks into that meeting as an authority. They leave it as one.

A system is a set of elements, interconnected by relationships, producing a function over time. Every organization is a system. Every team is a system. Every product is a system.

Systems thinking is the practice of analyzing problems at the level of structure — not incident. When something goes wrong repeatedly, a systems thinker asks: what is the structure producing this pattern? What relationships, incentives, or feedback loops are creating this outcome over and over?

My leadership framework — people, process, platform, performance — is a systems model. When a team is underperforming, you don't diagnose the symptom. You diagnose which of the four systems is producing the symptom.

Bad outcomes from unclear ownership? That's a process problem. Recurring quality failures? Could be people, could be platform, could be a performance standard that's not explicit. High attrition? Fix the system. The symptom resolves.

The conflicts weren't a discipline problem. They weren't a communication problem. They were a visibility problem built into the system's architecture.

The fix: a communication layer showing real-time editing status on every page. The incident rate dropped immediately. Not because people worked harder or followed better rules — because the system no longer produced the conflict conditions.

## The Leverage Point

Systems thinking changes where you invest effort. The highest-leverage interventions are almost always structural. Changing an incentive system produces more lasting change than changing individual behavior. Redesigning a process produces more lasting change than training people to work around a broken one.

The question to ask when a problem recurs: what is the structure producing this? Not who failed. Not what was misunderstood. What is the structure?

Find that structure. Change it. The problem stops recurring.

That's leverage. That's how one engineer with the right mental model outproduces a whole team treating symptoms.

The team didn't just learn to fix accessibility issues. They learned to see them — the way an experienced designer sees visual hierarchy, or an experienced developer sees security vulnerabilities. The domain became a professional capability, not a compliance task.

## The Tooling Investment

No compliance program works without tooling. I worked with SiteImprove developers to customize QA reports specific to our authoring team's workflow. I standardized how we used the Axe dev tool. I created a testing protocol that made accessibility checks a natural part of the page delivery process, not a separate audit step.

The principle: make the right behavior the easy behavior. Don't ask people to add steps — integrate the check into the existing process.

## The Cultural Signal

The moment I knew the program was working: a junior developer caught an accessibility issue in a senior developer's work during code review and flagged it without hesitation.

That moment — someone early in their career holding the standard against someone more senior — means the standard has been genuinely internalized. It's not compliance. It's culture.

That's the outcome worth building. Not passing an audit. Building a team that wouldn't ship inaccessible work even if you asked them to.

title: The Voice of the Educator — Teaching Complex Systems to Anyone
description: The ability to translate technical complexity into understanding anyone can act on is one of the most valuable and rare professional skills. Here's how I approach it.
date: '2026-05-16'
pillar: Excellence and Growth
---

I've taught every age group from three years old to eighty-nine. Not all of them were in classrooms or conference rooms — some were in living rooms, some were on the street, some were in coma recovery. The range is wide enough that I've had to learn one thing deeply: understanding how someone learns is more important than knowing what you're teaching.

That insight has shaped how I communicate in every professional context since.

## The Three Layers of Technical Communication

When I explain a technical system — an AI architecture, a content management workflow, an accessibility standard, a data pipeline — I move through three layers, in order:

**Layer 1: The problem it solves.** Not the technology, not the implementation — the human problem that motivated building it. "This exists because someone was experiencing [X] and needed a better way." Starting from the problem orients the listener before any technical information arrives.

**Layer 2: The mental model.** A simplified, accurate representation of how the system works. Not complete — useful. A metaphor or analogy that creates an accurate intuition without requiring full technical depth. "Think of it like a librarian who has read everything but can only answer questions they've been asked."

**Layer 3: The interaction surface.** What you need to understand to use this effectively. Not the full implementation — the inputs, outputs, and decisions that are yours to make.

Most technical communicators skip to Layer 3. The people in the room nod and leave having understood nothing they'll be able to act on.

## Adapting to the Learner

I adapt my teaching style to the person in front of me. Not a performance — a genuine recalibration of what frame will land.

With a senior developer: I skip the analogy and go deep on implementation decisions. They don't need the simplified model — they want the tradeoffs.

With an executive: I stay at Layer 1 almost entirely. What problem does this solve, what does success look like, what decision do they need to make?

With a new team member: I go slowly through all three layers, check understanding explicitly, and make it safe to ask the question twice.

The mistake most experts make: they stay in the mode that's comfortable for them, not the one that serves the learner.

## The Educator's Obligation

My deepest desire is to share what is in my heart and soul by any means — and I have a natural ability to understand complex systems and adapt my teaching style to the understanding of my students.

That ability is a responsibility. Not just a professional tool — an obligation to make the knowledge available to the person who needs it, in the form they can actually receive.

The knowledge that stays in your head is the knowledge that doesn't compound. Share it. Teach it. Watch what happens when the people around you understand at the level you do.

That's when the real work becomes possible.

title: What 10 Years of Enterprise CMS Taught Me About Building AI Systems
description: The lessons from managing 5,000 web pages at enterprise scale translate directly into the principles that govern well-architected AI systems.
date: '2026-05-17'
pillar: AI Engineering
---

I spent ten years in Adobe Experience Manager. At U.S. Bank, I coordinated 65 developers across 5,000 pages and 20 product teams. I built DAM governance systems, content fragment libraries, multi-site management processes, and accessibility compliance programs.

At the time, I thought this was my CMS career. Now I understand: it was my AI architecture training.

The principles that make enterprise content systems work at scale are the same principles that make AI systems reliable, maintainable, and trustworthy.

## Lesson 1: Governance Before Scale

The most common failure mode I saw in CMS deployments: organizations moved fast, skipped governance, and ended up with a DAM full of duplicate assets, inconsistent naming conventions, and siloed content that couldn't be found or reused.

The fix was always the same: establish governance before you scale. Naming conventions, folder structures, ownership models, intake standards — set them up when the team is small. They're cheap to implement early and nearly impossible to retrofit later.

The AI equivalent: data governance, prompt standards, output quality standards — define them before you scale the system. The RAG architecture that works for 10 documents behaves differently with 10,000. The prompt that produces good results with three users produces unpredictable results with three hundred.

Govern first. Scale second.

## Lesson 2: Content Fragments Are APIs

One of the best architectural decisions in AEM is the content fragment: structured content stored separately from presentation, reusable across channels, maintained in one place and consumed in many.

The principle: separate content from presentation. Don't couple the data to the display.

In AI systems: separate the knowledge layer from the interaction layer. Don't embed facts in prompts — put them in a retrieval system. Don't hard-code context — make it composable. The AI system that can update its knowledge without redeploying the application is architecturally healthier than one that can't.

Same principle, different domain.

## Lesson 3: Multi-Site Management Is Multi-Tenant Architecture

Managing content across 12 regional sites at AAA Insurance required a system that could share components, respect regional variation, and maintain governance across the entire estate simultaneously.

That's multi-tenant architecture. Different tenants, shared infrastructure, isolated contexts, centralized governance.

Every multi-user AI product is a multi-tenant architecture problem. Shared model, isolated context, per-tenant data isolation. The failure modes are identical: tenant data leaking, shared context contaminating individual sessions, governance at the estate level conflicting with flexibility at the tenant level.

The enterprise CMS instinct — build isolation in, don't retrofit it — is the right instinct for AI systems too.

## The Transfer

If you have enterprise system experience — CMS, ERP, CRM, any system that has to manage complexity at scale across multiple stakeholders — you have more AI engineering intuition than you think.

The domain vocabulary is different. The underlying architectural principles are not.

Bring your scale experience. Apply it deliberately. The AI systems that survive contact with real organizations are built by people who've already learned those lessons somewhere else.

title: The Compound Learning Strategy — Why What You Learn Now Multiplies Later
description: Most professionals treat learning as linear — more input, more output. Compound learning is different. Here's how to build a learning system that accelerates itself.
date: '2026-05-19'
pillar: Excellence and Growth

Einstein — or someone plausibly attributed to Einstein — called compound interest the eighth wonder of the world. The same principle applies to learning, and the professionals who understand this early build advantages that become impossible to close later.

Compound learning is not about learning more. It's about learning in a way that makes the next round of learning faster, deeper, and more connected.

## The Linear Model vs. the Compound Model

Most professional learning is linear. Read the documentation. Complete the course. Attend the training. Each unit of learning is relatively independent. The effort is roughly proportional to the output.

Compound learning is different. It builds a lattice of connected knowledge where new information connects to existing knowledge, which creates richer understanding, which makes the next new piece easier to acquire and more powerful once acquired.

The neuroscience is straightforward: learning is physically easier when it connects to prior knowledge. The connections are the compound return.

## Building the Lattice

The key discipline in compound learning: never learn in isolation. Always connect new knowledge to something you already know.

I do this explicitly. When I'm learning a new technology — Cloudflare Workers, for example — I don't just learn the API. I ask: how is this similar to things I already know? How does the edge runtime model relate to my mental model of request/response cycles from AEM dispatcher? What principles from my experience with multi-site CMS architecture apply here?

The connections I build are real, specific, and they make the new knowledge stick and integrate faster.

## The Documentation Advantage

Knowledge that you've articulated is more accessible, more durable, and more useful than knowledge that lives only in your head.

Writing what you learn forces you to understand it well enough to explain it. The explaining process surfaces the gaps — the things you thought you understood and didn't. And the written artifact is available to you, and others, in ways that mental knowledge isn't.

I document everything I build and everything I learn while building it. Not for an audience — for future me. The notes I wrote on a problem I solved in February are worth hours of re-learning in September.

## The AI Amplifier

This is where personal AI infrastructure enters: an AI that knows your existing knowledge lattice can accelerate the connection-building process.

When I'm learning something new, I use my AI to explicitly ask: how does this connect to what I already know? What are the analogies to systems I've already built? Where are the gaps in my understanding that would prevent me from applying this correctly?

That conversation doesn't replace the learning — it compresses it. The connections that would take weeks of application to develop through experience can be surfaced in hours through deliberate inquiry.

Learn in connections. Document as you go. Use AI to accelerate the lattice.

The compound returns start small and grow in ways that are hard to predict from the outside.

Build the learning system. Not the credential — the system.
